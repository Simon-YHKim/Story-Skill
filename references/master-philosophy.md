# Master Philosophy Reference (거장들의 작법 — 매체 횡단 통합)

**Read this at the start of any major project, and revisit when stuck.** This is the philosophical core of the skill — the cross-medium synthesis of how masters across literature, film/TV, and games actually work. The other reference files (narrative-craft, setup-payoff, character-deep-build, style-modes) are *tools*; this file is the *stance* from which the tools are wielded.

The eight masters profiled here (Dostoevsky, Morrison, Han Kang, Sorkin, Gilligan, Miyazaki, Druckmann, Kurvitz) work in different media but share the same underlying philosophy. The skill draws its standards from their *intersection*, not their differences.

## 1. The Three Universal Pillars (보편 3축)

Beneath every master across every medium, three pillars hold:

### Pillar 1 — Pathological Tracing of Self-Justification
The fundamental driver of well-made narrative is the **contradiction inside human psychology**, specifically: *how a character internally justifies their own choices, especially extreme ones*. Masters refuse flat morality and instead trace the moral self-deception by which characters keep operating.
- Dostoevsky's Raskolnikov justifying murder via Übermensch theory
- Vince Gilligan's Walter White justifying his fall via family / pride / "I am the one who knocks"
- Han Kang's perpetrators of state violence describing duty
- Kurvitz's Disco-Elysium protagonist whose every skill is a different rationalization

**The skill's stance**: every meaningful character must be able to deliver a first-person monologue defending their worst choice in their own internal logic. If they cannot, they are an archetype, not a character.

### Pillar 2 — Flawless Causal Architecture
No coincidence rescues the plot. Every consequence flows from a character's choice and the residue of prior events. Externals (weather, accidents, found objects) may *complicate* but may not *resolve*.
- Gilligan's "no clockwork-universe rescues" rule, enforced by physical index cards
- Aristotle's hamartia → peripeteia → anagnorisis chain
- Kurvitz's micro-reactivity (every dialogue choice flips boolean switches that compound)

**The skill's stance**: the Setup-Payoff Matrix is the digital index card. Every setup tracks a payoff; every payoff is rooted; coincidence is forbidden as resolution mechanic.

### Pillar 3 — Designed Absence (Audience as Co-Author)
Masters trust the audience and subtract. The work is what survives subtraction. The audience does cognitive and emotional work in the gaps, and that work is *what makes the work feel earned*.
- Morrison's "invisible ink" — the words the reader must surface from between the lines
- Hemingway's iceberg — seven-eighths below
- Miyazaki's "co-writing" — the joy of filling unclear text with imagination
- Sorkin's overlap and interruption — what gets cut off matters more than what gets said

**The skill's stance**: this is Principle 5. The 30% subtraction pass enforces it. Productive absence requires sufficient surrounding trace; lazy gaps require restoration.

## 2. The Five Master Techniques (거장의 작법 5종)

### 2.1 Pathological Self-Justification (Dostoevsky's Method)

**The technique**: rather than studying *what people did*, study *how they explained it to themselves*. Dostoevsky's source material was newspaper crime chronicles, but he extracted *psychological logic*, not facts. He was famously obsessed with Pierre-François Lacenaire — a Parisian intellectual murderer who defended his crimes philosophically at trial. Dostoevsky translated Lacenaire's trial transcripts personally for his magazine *Vremya*, not because the murder was interesting but because Lacenaire's *self-justification machinery* was.

**What this looks like in practice**:
- The character does not say "I did wrong." The character says "I did it because [internally consistent reasoning that still lets them sleep]."
- The reader sees the reasoning *function* before the reader sees it *fail*.
- The collapse of the rationalization, when it comes, is the climax — not the act itself.

**Apply to game writing**:
- Before writing any antagonist, write their first-person rationalization paragraph. Make it *almost* convincing — the reader should be uncomfortable agreeing partway.
- For protagonist arcs involving moral compromise, plant the rationalization early and let it crack across acts.
- Cross-reference: this is the *self-justification monologue worksheet* in `references/character-deep-build.md`.

**The diagnostic question**: if you removed the character's rationalization, would what remains still be a coherent person? If yes, the rationalization is decoration. If no — if the rationalization *is the spine* — you have a character.

### 2.2 Invisible Ink and Rememory (Morrison's Method)

**The technique**: write what is on the page, *and simultaneously* write what is *not* on the page in a way that makes the audience surface it. Morrison called the second layer "invisible ink." She also called the act of doing this "rememory" — *re-assembling members of the past that have been scattered*.

The technique came from her conviction that imitating Western (white, male) narrative conventions was a "dim-witted" approach for an African-American novelist. She built her own form by:
1. Reading slave narratives and oral histories — un-fictionalized first-person testimony
2. Editing *The Black Book* (1974) — 18 months of curating Black historical artifacts
3. Finding Margaret Garner's news clipping inside that work — a real woman who killed her child to prevent re-enslavement — and letting that fragment ferment for years before *Beloved*

**Two specific Morrison moves**:
1. **Trauma is not narrated; it is staged at sensory distance**. The book describes a torn dress, a song, a smell — and the reader assembles the violence themselves.
2. **The chronology is broken on purpose**. Rememory works *associatively*, not linearly, because trauma works that way. The reader's confusion is the point — they reconstruct what the character cannot bear to reconstruct directly.

**Apply to game writing**:
- For backstory reveals, never write the wound directly. Write three sensory anchors connected to it across the game. The player's mind connects them.
- For atrocity, war, loss — never describe the act. Describe the room before, the room after, and one specific surviving object.
- For ensemble worlds, scatter the narrative deliberately. Trust the player to assemble.
- Borrow Morrison's *fragment method* for research: collect three real-world un-fictionalized fragments related to the project before writing fiction. Let them ferment.

### 2.3 Embodied Metaphor (Han Kang's Method)

**The technique**: when a phenomenon is abstract — trauma, history, oppression, fate — find the **physical/biological metaphor** that lets the abstract live in the body of the prose. The metaphor must be *materially specific* to the world: not "haunting" (cliché) but a precise physical analog.

The signature example: while researching *Human Acts*, Han Kang interviewed survivors of the Gwangju massacre's torture chambers. One survivor said: *"The experience was like radiation exposure."* Han built her novel's central metaphor from this — state violence as a substance that lodges in marrow, alters chromosomes, and continues to mutate descendants for decades. The trauma is not described as trauma; it is described as **slow-acting radiation**, and the prose carries that physical weight.

**Why this works**:
- Abstract description recruits the reader's intellect. Physical metaphor recruits the reader's body.
- A reader can *imagine* radiation in marrow. They cannot really imagine "lasting trauma."
- The metaphor unifies otherwise disparate scenes — once "radiation" is the metaphor, every scene about an aftermath inherits the radiation logic.

**Apply to game writing**:
- For any thematic element with an abstract noun (memory, loss, faith, decay, oppression, hope), do not write the abstract noun. Find the *physical thing it is most like in this world*, and let the world describe itself in those terms.
- Worldbuilding shortcut: if a culture has a "central cultural anxiety" (Principle in worldbuilding section), find the physical metaphor that culture uses to describe the anxiety to itself. ("The Plague Whispers" / "the Salt in the Bones" / "the Silence-Sickness".)
- For trauma in a character's backstory, do not describe the trauma. Describe what *kind of physical residue* the trauma left, and let scene after scene rediscover that residue in different forms.

**The test**: a fully embodied metaphor lets a writer add new scenes that automatically extend the metaphor without needing thematic explanation. If you can write a new scene with the metaphor in mind and it fits without forcing, the metaphor is working.

### 2.4 Intention and Obstacle (Sorkin's Method)

**The technique**: every scene with two or more characters is a clash of *specific, conscious goals* against *specific obstacles*. Sorkin treats dialogue as music ("the words sound" matters as much as "what the words mean"), but the music only works if the underlying scene has a fundamental goal-collision driving it.

Sorkin's claim, in his MasterClass: *"Every scene needs intention and obstacle. The character has to want something specific — *not* 'to be happy' or 'to be loved' but *something they could fail at right now in this scene*. Then something has to be in the way."*

**Sorkin's secondary technique**: rhythm. He writes with an awareness of iambic stress, sentence-length variance, and overlap (interruption) that approximates how people *fight* with words. But he is explicit: rhythm is the surface; intention/obstacle is the structure. Without the structure, beautiful rhythm produces nothing.

**Apply to game writing**:
- Before writing any dialogue scene, write two lines: **"Character A wants ___ from this scene."** and **"Character B wants ___ from this scene."** If A and B want the same thing, the scene is dead. If either wants nothing specific, the scene is dead.
- The obstacle does not have to be the other character. Sometimes the obstacle is the room (a dying parent in earshot), the time (the train leaves in five minutes), the institution (we cannot say this here). The obstacle just has to be *real and specific*.
- Then, on top of the goal-collision, layer rhythm. Long sentences for control. Fragments for breaking. Overlap when characters cannot wait their turn. One-word lines after long silence for collapse.
- Subtext is a *consequence* of intention/obstacle: when a character cannot say their goal directly (because the obstacle includes social cost), they say something *else* that carries the goal's pressure indirectly.

**The diagnostic question**: if you swapped any line of dialogue between two of your characters, would the scene still make sense? If yes, the characters are not in distinct intentions. Each line should be unmovable from its speaker.

### 2.5 Index-Card Causality (Gilligan's Method)

**The technique**: every plot beat is physicalized as an index card on a corkboard, and *no card is allowed to remain on the board unless it passes two questions*:
1. **"What's in this character's head right now?"** (i.e., this beat must be motivated by *internal* state, not authorial convenience)
2. **"What happens next *because of* this?"** (i.e., this beat must causally generate the next beat, not be followed by something accidentally)

If a card cannot answer both, it is removed. The corkboard becomes the spine of the season *before any script is written*.

This is why *Breaking Bad* feels causally inevitable — the writers' room rejected, on principle, "and then a meth lab fire happens" or "and then a rat eats the money." Every catastrophe is *Walter White's choice having a consequence Walter White did not foresee*. The clockwork-universe trap (random event drives plot) was the explicit enemy.

**Two underrated parts of the system**:
- **Writers' room is collaborative argument**, not voting. Gilligan describes it as a place where the *most embarrassing version of the question is preferred over the polite version*. "Wait, why does Walt do this? That's stupid." → forces the room to either justify it from internal state or remove it.
- **Verisimilitude through real research**. The chemistry in *Breaking Bad* is real. The legal procedure in *Better Call Saul* is real. The cards have to survive both motivational logic *and* world-truth. Gilligan called this "the MacGyver aspect."

**Apply to game writing**:
- The Setup-Payoff Matrix in `references/setup-payoff.md` is this technique digitalized. Use it.
- For every plot beat: *write the character's interior state* before *writing the scene*. If the interior state is generic, fix the interior state, not the scene.
- For long-form projects: maintain a literal sequence of beats with two columns — "interior driver" and "consequence". A beat with empty columns gets cut.
- For genre / world plausibility, do the research. The cracked teacup needs to be a real kind of teacup; the political situation needs to be a real political situation; the mythology needs to internally consistent. Lazy worldbuilding produces lazy plot logic.

## 3. The Three Game-Medium Innovations (게임 매체 3대 혁신)

The previous five techniques apply across all narrative media. The next three are *specific to interactive media* and represent how games extend storytelling beyond what film or prose can do.

### 3.1 Co-Writing Through Absence (Miyazaki's Method)

**The technique**: deliberately reduce narrative resolution so the player becomes a co-author. Lore appears in *item descriptions*, *environmental composition*, *NPC fragments*, *architectural ruins* — never in cutscene exposition. The player reconstructs the world through interpretation.

Miyazaki traces this to his childhood reading of fantasy novels above his reading level: he could only understand fragments, and he filled the gaps with imagination. He realized he had been *co-writing* the books. He designs games to give players the same experience.

**Why this is more than just "subtle":**
- The world must *fully exist* in the designer's head, even if only fragments are shown. Players reconstruct, and they will catch incoherence. *Dark Souls* has consistent metaphysics; players have written entire wikis. The lore is real; only the *delivery* is fragmented.
- The fragments must be **densely meaningful** — every item description does narrative work. *Bloodborne*'s "Old Hunter Bone" carries decades of lore in two sentences. Lazy item flavor breaks the system harder than no item flavor.
- The architecture is part of the storytelling. *Bloodborne*'s gothic verticality, the way Yharnam's districts transition by class — these *are* the narrative.

**Apply to game writing**:
- For environmental storytelling: every named location must answer both **"what happened here?"** and **"what does it mean for *this* player to be the one finding this?"**. The latter question is what separates atmosphere from co-writing.
- For item lore: every item with flavor text must reward close reading. If the flavor text is filler, cut it. If it is real lore, make sure three separate items can be cross-referenced to triangulate a fact.
- For NPC dialogue: the character knows more than they say. The player should leave any conversation *aware they did not get the full story*, with traces sufficient to reconstruct.
- The discipline: **write the full lore privately, deliver only fragments publicly**. The fragments-to-lore ratio for a Souls-like is roughly 1:5 — for every page of dialogue or item flavor the player reads, the writer should have five pages of internal lore the player never sees directly.

### 3.2 Ludonarrative Resonance (Druckmann's Method)

**The technique**: gameplay verbs and narrative themes must align. *What the player does* and *what the story claims* must reinforce, not contradict, each other. Naughty Dog's Neil Druckmann is the most prominent designer of this discipline.

The classic problem (named "ludonarrative dissonance" by Clint Hocking): a story claims the protagonist is a reluctant survivor, but gameplay involves cheerfully mass-murdering hundreds. The mismatch breaks immersion.

Druckmann's *The Last of Us* example: he originally wrote 14-year-old Ellie as a non-combatant who only picks up a weapon at the climax. Playtesting revealed players couldn't bond emotionally with a non-active companion. Druckmann rewrote both story and mechanics so that Ellie helps in stealth gameplay much earlier — mechanically and narratively integrated.

His principle: **"Cutscenes are a last resort."** A cutscene is what you use when no in-game interaction can deliver the moment. Every moment that *can* be played should be played, because player participation makes the moment land harder than passive viewing.

**Apply to game writing**:
- When designing a major story beat, ask: **what verb does the player do during this beat, and does that verb match the theme?** A grief beat where the player verb is "shoot" is dissonant. A grief beat where the player verb is "carry" or "look" or "fail to act" is resonant.
- For thematic worlds: identify the **central verb-theme mapping** at project start. *Spiritfarer* is about "ferrying" — a verb that maps to grief work. *Disco Elysium* is about "noticing" — a verb that maps to perception of the social. *Outer Wilds* is about "knowing" — a verb that maps to mortality and inheritance. What is *your* game's verb? If the answer is "shooting things," the theme will collapse to "violence" no matter what the dialogue claims.
- Write cutscenes only for moments the camera *must* see — micro-expressions, framing only the director can control. Anything that can be played, play.

### 3.3 Failure as Content (Kurvitz's Method)

**The technique**: in branching narrative, **failure paths must produce new narrative**, not lock-out screens. A failed dice roll, a missed opportunity, a refused conversation — each branches into its own story, often more interesting than the success branch.

Kurvitz, in interviews about *Disco Elysium*: *"I wanted to accept any stupid thing the player does."* The team treated failure not as "the player chose wrong" but as "the player chose *a different story*." A failed Composure check doesn't lock content — it opens a tragicomic moment of public humiliation that often contains the game's best writing.

The deeper principle: **the player is not opposed to failure**. The player is opposed to *failure that erases their experience*. Failure that *creates* experience is welcomed.

The technical implementation: every check, dialogue branch, and skill challenge has *both* a success path and a failure path written in full. The Thought Cabinet system extends this — even the player's *ideologies* (communism, fascism, moralism) are mechanical-narrative content. There is no "wrong" build; there are different books being written.

**Apply to game writing**:
- For every player choice with a fail state, write the fail-state scene as if it were the canonical scene. Often it should be funnier, sadder, or more revealing than the success.
- For dialogue trees: "do nothing" / "say nothing" / "leave" must be a real content path, not an absence of content. The player's silence is its own story.
- For long arcs: the player's worst possible playthrough should still be a *coherent and interesting story*, not a broken mess. This is the discipline.
- The author's stance: **bless the player's bad choices**. The player who roleplays a coward, a fool, or a moral disaster is offering you a different story to tell. Tell it.

## 4. The Skill's Working Stance — How the Pillars Become Behavior

These eight masters are not a checklist; they are a **stance**. The skill operates as if all eight are watching every page. Concretely:

| When the skill is doing... | It asks itself... |
|---|---|
| Writing any antagonist | Could this character deliver Dostoevsky's first-person rationalization? |
| Describing trauma or abstraction | What is the Han Kang embodied metaphor for this in *this* world? |
| Designing a scene with two characters | What is the Sorkin intention of each, and what is the obstacle? |
| Plotting an arc | Does every Gilligan card answer "what's in their head" + "what happens next"? |
| Writing a backstory reveal | Is this Morrison invisible ink, or am I describing the wound directly? |
| Designing a location | Does this Miyazaki location answer "what happened here?" *and* "what does it mean for me to find it?" |
| Designing a story beat | What is Druckmann's verb here, and does it resonate with the theme? |
| Writing a choice or fail state | Is the Kurvitz fail path as rich as the success path? |
| Finishing a draft | Where is the 30% I should subtract? |

When the skill cannot answer one of these questions for a piece of work, the work is not yet done. Go back and answer it.

## 5. Practical Application Guide

### 5.1 Project Start Checklist (do these in order)

1. **Find or generate three non-fiction fragments** related to the project's core themes. (Court records, oral histories, news items, real cultural objects.) Do not move forward without them.
2. **Write the embodied metaphor sentence** for the central thematic element. (*"Grief in this world is like ___ that ___."*)
3. **Write the protagonist's self-justification monologue** — first person, defending their worst choice in their internal logic.
4. **Identify the central verb-theme mapping**. (What does the player *do*, and what does that doing *mean*?)
5. **Write one paragraph of designed absence** — what will this work *not* explain, and how will the player reconstruct it?
6. **Begin the Setup-Payoff Matrix** with at least 5 entries.

Only after these are done should you write a synopsis or a scene. They take an hour each. They prevent weeks of wasted writing.

### 5.2 Mid-Project Diagnostic (when stuck)

If the project feels off, run through:

- **Pillar 1 check**: am I describing what characters *do*, or *how they justify what they do*?
- **Pillar 2 check**: is something happening because of the character's prior choice, or because the plot needs it to?
- **Pillar 3 check**: am I explaining what the audience could reconstruct?
- **Verb check**: does what the player does match what the story claims?
- **Subtraction check**: which 30% can I cut?

The answer to "I'm stuck" is almost always "I'm violating one of these." Identify which.

### 5.3 Pre-Delivery Audit

Before delivering any major piece:

- [ ] Self-justification monologue exists for every named character (or abbreviated version for minor)
- [ ] Embodied metaphor identified for central abstraction
- [ ] Setup-Payoff Matrix complete with no orphan entries
- [ ] Designed absence paragraph included for any synopsis or full work
- [ ] No deus ex machina in resolution
- [ ] Every dialogue scene has identifiable intention/obstacle for both speakers
- [ ] Verb-theme mapping is consistent across major beats
- [ ] Failure paths are content, not lock-out
- [ ] 30% subtraction pass completed
- [ ] At least one long-con foreshadow lands 10+ scenes after plant

If five or more boxes are unchecked, the work is not yet ready.

## Closing Note

The masters profiled here did not start as masters. Dostoevsky failed at a military career. Morrison was an editor for decades before publishing fiction. Miyazaki worked under FromSoft's previous leadership for years on games no one remembers. Sorkin had a years-long cocaine problem during *Sports Night*. Kurvitz spent fifteen years on a TRPG before *Disco Elysium*.

What unites them is not talent — it is *stance*. They learned to look at human contradiction, refuse coincidence, and trust the audience. The skill exists to encode that stance into game-writing practice.

When in doubt, return to the three pillars: **trace self-justification, refuse coincidence, design absence**. Everything else is technique.
