# Character Deep Build Reference (캐릭터 정밀 빌드)

Read this for any character creation task. The standard: every named character should be distinguishable from every other, with interior depth that supports both their own arc and their structural function in the larger work. Depth-per-character over breadth.

This reference integrates formal character theory (want vs. need, the lie they believe, the ghost) with practical worksheets and a 7-layer template.

## Foundational Concepts

### Want vs. Need (Robert McKee)
- **Want**: the conscious external goal the character would name aloud — "I want my father's approval", "I want the throne", "I want to find who killed my brother"
- **Need**: the unconscious internal transformation required for the character to be whole — "I need to stop seeking approval to be free", "I need to admit I do not want power", "I need to forgive myself for not preventing the death"
- **The arc lives in the gap.** A great character usually does not get exactly what they wanted — they discover what they needed (or refuse it).

### The Lie They Believe (Truby)
The false belief installed by the wound. The character's defining behaviors are attempts to be true to this lie.
- "If I love, I will lose."
- "If I am seen completely, I will be abandoned."
- "Power is the only protection."
- "My worth depends on my use to others."

The arc shape:
- **Positive arc**: lie dismantled, character grows into truth
- **Negative arc**: lie reinforced, character clings harder, falls
- **Flat arc**: character holds truth from the start, changes the world (or fails to)
- **Disillusionment arc**: starts believing a benign lie, comes to believe a darker truth

### The Ghost (the wound)
The specific past event that installed the lie. Best practice: never fully shown until late, but *felt* throughout in avoidance behaviors and triggered reactions. The character's behavior should be readable as "shaped by something specific" without that specific being named.

### The Single-Line Paradox
The character's gravity center — one sentence that contains a productive contradiction. Refuse the character until you can write this line.

Examples:
- "가장 다정하기 때문에 가장 잔인할 수 있는 사람."
- "용서받기 위해 평생 사과하지 않은 사람."
- "사랑하는 모든 것을 떠나보내는 것으로 사랑을 증명하는 사람."
- "거짓말 한 번을 위해 평생을 진실하게 산 사람."
- "자신을 가장 미워하는 사람만이 자신을 견딜 수 있다고 믿는 사람."

If you cannot write the paradox, you have a sketch, not a character. Go back to wound + lie + want/need.

## The 7-Layer Character Sheet

Every named character should pass through all seven. Skip a layer and the character flattens.

### Layer 1 — 표면 (Surface)
What the world sees. Profession, public role, what they say at parties. **2–3 sentences max.** This is the recruiting-poster version.

### Layer 2 — 음성 (Voice)
How they speak — the fingerprint test. Could you identify a line of dialogue as theirs without a name tag?
- **Rhythm**: controlled / breaking / performing / defensive / sparse / dialect / coded
- **Vocabulary register**: which class of words do they reach for? (한자어 vs. 토박이말, formal vs. casual, technical vs. lyrical)
- **Signature tic**: one specific verbal habit (a phrase they overuse, a question they ask instead of answering, a way they begin sentences)
- **Avoidance**: one phrase, topic, or name they will not say out loud
- **Sample line**: one short paragraph proving the rhythm

### Layer 3 — 신체 (Body)
Specific physical signatures. Not "tall and handsome" — load-bearing details.
- **Habit**: one gesture under pressure (tucks hair behind left ear, presses thumbnail into palm, taps the table three times before speaking)
- **Mark**: one physical trace with a story (a scar, a callus, a hand that won't fully close, a slight limp on cold days)
- **Spatial pattern**: how they occupy a room (sit at edge or center? coat on or off? back to wall or door?)
- Avoid generic appearance lists. Three specific details > a paragraph of description.

### Layer 4 — 결정적 상처 (Defining Wound / The Ghost)
The specific event that installed the lie. Required elements:
- **When**: time of day, season, year of life
- **Where**: specific place
- **Who**: the other person involved (wounds in well-made fiction are almost always relational)
- **Sensory anchor**: smell, weather, sound, light quality
- **The unspoken sentence**: what one of them did not say but should have

Vague trauma is not character. "His parents died young" does nothing. "His mother died on a Tuesday in May, and the last thing she asked for was tea, and he had not yet learned to brew it properly" *does* something — and it loads three potential setups (Tuesdays, May weather, tea-making) for later recovery.

### Layer 5 — 욕망 (Want)
What they would consciously name as their goal. Often misdirected.

### Layer 6 — 두려움 / 필요 (Fear / Need)
- **Fear**: what the wound made them afraid of — usually the *inverse* of the want
- **Need**: what they would have to accept or do to actually be whole — usually requires facing the fear
- Pattern: "She wants to be loved unconditionally. She fears being seen completely. She needs to risk being seen by one person and accept whatever comes."

### Layer 7 — 비밀 (Secret) / The Lie They Believe
- **The Lie**: the false belief installed by the wound, stated as a sentence the character would (privately) defend
- **The Secret**: the never-admitted fact about themselves they have told no one
- These are linked: the secret is usually evidence the character interprets as proof of the lie

## Want / Need / Lie / Ghost Worksheet

For every named character, fill this:

```
NAME: [한글 / Romanization]

PARADOX (one line):

GHOST (the wound — when, where, with whom, sensory anchor, unspoken sentence):

LIE (the false belief installed):

WANT (conscious goal):

FEAR (inverse of want, or what the lie defends against):

NEED (what truth the character must face for arc to resolve):

ARC TYPE (positive / negative / flat / disillusionment):

ARC TURN (the specific moment in the story where the character either accepts the need or refuses it — name the scene):

GRAVITY CHARACTER (which other character is the magnetic north of this one's interior):

SELF-JUSTIFICATION MONOLOGUE (one short first-person paragraph in which the character defends their worst choice using their own internal logic — see next section):

DESIGNED ABSENCE (one fact about this character that the audience should never be told outright, but should be reconstructable from behavior across at least three scenes):
```

If any field is blank, the character is not built. If any field is generic ("trauma", "to find love", "selfishness"), it is not specific enough.

## The Self-Justification Monologue (Dostoevsky's Diagnostic)

This is the single most reliable test for whether you have built a character or assembled an archetype. **A built character can deliver this monologue; an assembled archetype cannot.**

### The Test
Write one short first-person paragraph (4–8 sentences) in which this character defends their worst choice — the act they have most reason to be ashamed of, or that society would most condemn — using *their own internal logic*. The voice must be the character's voice, not yours. The reasoning must be **almost convincing** — the reader should be uncomfortable agreeing partway, the way Dostoevsky makes us uncomfortable inside Raskolnikov's reasoning before the structure collapses.

### What the monologue must contain
1. **The character names what they did** without minimizing — they call it what it is, in their own register
2. **They give the reasoning that lets them sleep** — the chain of internal logic that converts the act from "wrong" to "necessary" or "earned" or "the only choice"
3. **They reveal the wound underneath** — without naming it, the monologue carries traces of why this rationalization specifically is the one this person needed
4. **They show what would break the rationalization** — implicitly, by what they cannot face

### Worked example

**Character**: 50대 약방 주인, 23년 전 동생을 의금부에 잃었음. 동생을 끌고 간 도사가 지금 자기 약방의 단골이고, 그를 죽이지 않은 자신을 매일 미워한다.

**Self-justification monologue:**
> 내가 그놈을 살려둔 게 자비라고? 아니. 자비는 그놈이 받을 자격이 없어. 내가 그놈을 안 죽인 건 — 죽이면 내가 그놈처럼 되는 거니까. 동생이 끌려가던 그날, 우리 어머니가 마당에서 무릎을 꿇고 빌었어. 사람을 살려달라고. 그 어머니의 아들이 사람을 죽이면, 그 빎이 거짓말이 되는 거야. 그래서 내가 매일 그놈한테 약을 지어주는 거지. 약을 지어주면서, 그놈 손목에 내가 달인 약이 들어가는 걸 보면서, 나는 어머니의 약속을 지키고 있는 거야. ... 그리고 어쩌면, 그놈이 언젠가 알아차리길 바라는 건지도 모르지. 내가 누군지를. 자기가 지금 누구한테 절을 하고 있는지를.

What this monologue does:
- Names the choice (살려둔 것)
- Gives a moral-philosophical reasoning that *almost* works (혈통의 약속을 지키는 것)
- Reveals the wound (어머니의 빎)
- Shows what would break it (마지막 문장 — 사실 자비가 아니라 *고문*이라는 자각이 새어 나옴)

A character who can speak like this has interior. A character who can only say "I want revenge but I won't take it" is a label.

### Failure modes — when the monologue is wrong

- **Too clean**: the character sounds like they have figured everything out. Real rationalization has *cracks* — moments where the speaker almost admits the truth, then pulls back.
- **Too defensive**: the character argues with an imagined accuser. A true self-justification is *spoken to oneself*, not at a critic.
- **Too generic**: the reasoning could belong to anyone with a similar wound. The wound's specific shape must show in the specific *texture* of the reasoning.
- **Too articulate**: not every character is verbally fluent. A taciturn dock worker should rationalize in a taciturn dock worker's voice — fragmented, gestural, allergic to abstraction. The voice constraint is not optional.
- **Author intrusion**: the writer's own moral framework leaks in. If the monologue contains "what I should have done" or "I know I was wrong," the author is in the room. Get out.

### When to write this monologue

- For protagonists: at character creation, before any scene is written
- For antagonists: at character creation, before any scene is written. *Always.* The antagonist's monologue is more important than the protagonist's; without it, you have a villain, not a character.
- For major supporting characters: at character creation
- For minor characters with one defining choice: write a 2–3 sentence abbreviated version
- For characters whose only function is exposition or service: not required (and arguably they are not characters; they are devices)

## Designed Absence — One Fact Never Stated

For each major character, identify **one fact** about them the audience should *never be told outright* but should be reconstructable from behavior across at least three scenes. This is the character-level application of Principle 5 (designed absence).

### Examples

- **The fact**: this character once betrayed their sibling for money.
  - **The traces**: they cannot hold a coin without setting it down quickly; they overreact when someone calls them generous; they send anonymous gifts to a family they have never met.
- **The fact**: this character cannot read.
  - **The traces**: they always ask others to read aloud "to share"; they sign documents only after long pauses; their library is full of books with uncut pages.
- **The fact**: this character was the one who started the fire that destroyed the village.
  - **The traces**: they refuse to light candles at festivals; they smell of smoke in the rain; they have memorized the names of every dead villager.

### Rules

- The fact must be **specific** (a single event or condition), not abstract ("they have a dark past")
- The traces must be **distributed** across at least three scenes — never concentrated, or the audience figures it out too quickly
- The traces must be **consistent with the surface character** — the audience should not realize they are seeing clues until they look back
- The fact, once reconstructed, must **recontextualize prior scenes**, not just add information

### Why this matters

A character with one designed absence has *interior the audience earns access to*. A character without one is only what the audience is told. The first feels like a person; the second feels like a description.

## Contradiction-Pair Generator

When stuck, pair a public face with its private inverse:

| Public face | Private truth |
|---|---|
| The cheerful caretaker | Has not slept through a night in 12 years |
| The cold strategist | Cries quietly while watching certain commercials |
| The reckless rebel | Has eaten the same conservative breakfast for a decade |
| The pious priest | Stopped believing the year his sister died |
| The seductive socialite | Has never been touched without flinching |
| The ironclad king | Writes letters he never sends to a brother who never existed |
| The loyal soldier | Memorizes every betrayal, executes none |
| The brilliant scholar | Cannot read certain words without his hands shaking |
| The healer who saves all | Failed to save the one that mattered |
| The drunk in the alley | Knows three languages and chose this exit |
| The merciful judge | Has signed one execution order he believes was wrong |
| The fearless explorer | Never returns to one specific city |

Use as starters. The pair must be *specifically yours* — refine until it points at *one* character, not a category.

## Speech Rhythm Samples

Every named character needs a distinguishable rhythm. Starting points:

### 1. Controlled (control hides cost)
> "그러시는군요. 알겠습니다. 결정은 본부장님께서 내리시는 거니까요. 다만, 한 가지만 여쭙겠습니다."

Long sentences, complete clauses, precise honorifics. Cracks when sentences shorten.

### 2. Breaking (fracture shows truth)
> "아니… 그게… 아니에요. 그냥… 됐어요."

Fragments, ellipses, retreats mid-sentence. Often appears after stretches of controlled speech — the contrast is the drama.

### 3. Performing (volume hides hollow)
> "야, 진짜 오랜만이다! 어떻게 지냈어? 야, 너 살 빠졌네? 부럽다 진짜!"

Exclamations, questions stacked, energy that doesn't pause for answers. This character never lets silence land.

### 4. Defensive (deflection as shell)
> "그건 그렇고. 밥은? 밥은 먹었어?"

Topic-changes, returns to mundane, deflects emotional weight onto care for the other person.

### 5. Sparse (silence as gravity)
> "…그래."

Short. Earned silences. Every word costs. When this character speaks two sentences in a row, the reader pays attention.

### 6. Dialect / register (origin as identity)
> "아따, 그라믄 안 되제. 우리 어무이 살아 계셨으믄 너 한 대 맞았다."

Sustained dialect signals where they came from and what they refused to leave behind.

### 7. Coded (formality as wound)
> "선생님께서 그리 말씀하신다면, 저는 그저 따를 뿐입니다."

Excessive formality is often armor. A character who speaks this way to a peer is hiding something specific.

### 8. Lyrical (interior leaks out)
> "오늘은 비가 왔지. 비 오는 날엔, 이상하게 누군가가 자꾸 떠올라. 이름은 안 나는데."

Associative, sensory, willing to stop the conversation for an image. The character's interior is too crowded to stay polite.

### 9. Interrogative (questions as defense / probe)
> "그래서요? 그게 무슨 뜻인지 정확히 좀… 어떻게 들으셨어요?"

Asks instead of answers. Either deflecting or testing — the context tells you which.

## Object Talisman Library

Every major character carries (or pointedly avoids) one physical object loaded with memory. Pick one and bind it to the wound.

| Object | Common connotation | Use it for |
|---|---|---|
| 손목시계 | Time someone gave them; time stopped | A character who survived someone |
| 만년필 | Promises written and unwritten | A character who failed to send a letter |
| 사진 한 장 | Frozen moment they cannot leave | A character who has stopped aging emotionally |
| 손수건 | Care from a vanished hand | A character whose mother is gone |
| 반지 | A vow, kept or broken | Make it specific (an engraving, a missing stone) |
| 책 한 권 | Words from someone now silent | A character carrying instructions they cannot follow |
| 열쇠 | A door they will not open | A character with a room they have not entered in years |
| 흉터 | A body that remembers | A character whose body knows what they refuse to recall |
| 음식 한 그릇 | A taste that summons a person | A character who cooks what they cannot eat |
| 노래 한 곡 | An ear that betrays the heart | A character who changes the radio when it plays |
| 편지 한 장 | The thing left unsent | A character who wrote the truth once and hid it |
| 동전 한 닢 | An odd / lucky / cursed token | A character with a private superstition |
| 깨진 도자기 | Repair as identity | A character who keeps repairing themselves |
| 책갈피 | A page never reached | A character with a story they cannot finish |

The object should appear in **at least three scenes**: introduction, midpoint deepening, and resolution. By the third appearance, the player should feel its weight without explanation. **This is also a setup-payoff entry** — track it on the matrix.

## Anti-Patterns to Refuse

These constructions are signs that character work has not been done — they are assembly, not building.

- **"Mysterious past"** — what *specifically* happened, with sensory detail, or it doesn't count
- **"Loyal to a fault"** — loyalty without conflict is decoration; what does this loyalty cost them, *specifically*?
- **"Tortured genius"** — genius alone is not character; what does the genius break that they cannot fix?
- **"Cold but caring deep down"** — show one specific small caring action; do not label the dichotomy
- **"Tragic backstory makes them strong"** — strength from trauma is the lazy arc; show *how* they walk wounded
- **"Unbreakable bond"** — bonds in well-made fiction must be tested specifically; "unbreakable" is the marketing version
- **"Just wants to be normal"** — what does *this character* think normal is? What about the wound makes them define it that way?
- **"Doesn't trust anyone after what happened"** — universal trauma response; what *specific* form does their distrust take that another character's would not?

If a character sketch reads like a TVTropes paragraph, it has been assembled, not built. Return to the wound + lie + want/need worksheet.

## Cast Coherence (when building multiple characters at once)

When generating multiple characters for a project, ensure cast-level coherence:

- **Distinct rhythms**: no two named characters should share a speech rhythm category
- **Thematic prism**: each character should refract the central theme differently (if the theme is "the cost of forgiveness", one character forgives too easily, one cannot forgive at all, one forgives in ways that wound, etc.)
- **Gravity network**: every character has at least one other character as their gravity well; ensure the network is interesting (not all gravity flowing toward the protagonist)
- **No two characters with the same lie**: each character's lie should be distinct, even if related thematically
- **One sketch per supporting role**: full 7-layer for major characters; abbreviated worksheet (paradox + want/need + lie + voice rhythm) for supporting roles
