# Korean Aesthetics Reference (한국식 서사 미학)

**Positioning**: This is *one style mode among many* (see `references/style-modes.md`), not the default voice of the skill. Read when the brief calls for Korean-drama or Korean-novel emotional register specifically — slow-burn relationships, jeong/han weight, the stillness scene as flagship moment, honorific shifts as plot events. The four operating principles (intricate weaving, setup-payoff precision, decoration-is-sin, multi-style mastery) hold across all modes; this reference adds the specific *technique vocabulary* of Korean dramatic tradition.

Use when:
- The user explicitly asks for Korean-drama / Korean-novel feel
- The setting is contemporary Korea or historical Korean periods
- The work has been pitched as "치밀한 감정선" / "K-드라마식" / "한국 정서"
- A specific scene needs the stillness-scene treatment regardless of overall style

## 1. 정 (Jeong) and 한 (Han) — Calibration Guide

### 정 (Jeong)
*Affective bond formed through shared time, food, hardship — survives even reasons to break.* This is why a Korean character forgives the unforgivable, or refuses to abandon a person who has clearly betrayed them. Jeong is **not** love in the Western sense; it is closer to "we have eaten too many meals across the same table."

**Signals of jeong on the page:**
- Cooking food the other character used to like, even after they're gone
- Returning to the same alley/staircase/bus stop without naming why
- The smaller character moves the bigger character's umbrella into the rain when the bigger character isn't looking
- "Why do you still..." — a question asked by someone who already knows why

**Signals of jeong in dialogue:**
- Refusing to use someone's title when alone with them
- Speaking in unfinished sentences the other character finishes
- Scolding that is actually worry ("밥은 먹었어?" said while angry)

### 한 (Han)
*Long-held grief that has hardened into part of identity.* Not just sadness — sadness with dignity, with the weight of years, often tied to historical injustice or generational wound. A character with han does not weep; they carry.

**Han on the page:**
- A character who has stopped naming what was taken from them
- Songs hummed without the lyrics
- The same daily ritual (boiling water, pouring tea) performed for someone who is not coming
- Dignity in the face of repeated humiliation — not stoicism, but a refusal to grant the humiliator the satisfaction

**Common mistake**: writing han as bitterness or vengefulness. Bitterness is loud; han is quiet. A vengeful character is using their wound; a character with han has been *shaped* by their wound until it cannot be separated from who they are.

## 2. The Stillness Scene — 정적 장면

A scene where almost nothing happens externally and everything happens internally. The signature move of Korean drama. The skill should produce at least one of these per major arc.

**Recipe:**
1. **Confined space** — a kitchen, a bus, a hospital corridor, a 평상 in summer.
2. **Two characters with unspoken history**, doing a mundane shared activity.
3. **One physical object** that carries the buried tension (a peeled apple slid across a table, a loose thread on a sleeve, a kettle that won't whistle).
4. **One line of dialogue that lands wrong** — something innocuous, but it cracks something.
5. **No resolution.** They go back to the activity. The reader/player carries the crack out of the scene.

**Bad version:** "They sat in silence. The tension was palpable." (telling, not showing)
**Good version:** Describe the apple peel falling in one continuous spiral. Describe who picks it up. Let the reader feel the held breath in the description's restraint.

## 3. Honorific Shifts as Plot Events

In Korean storytelling, a character switching from 존댓말 to 반말 (or vice versa) is a **plot event** equivalent to a kiss or a confession in Western narrative. Use sparingly. Each shift should:

- Be **earned** by what came before
- Be **noticed** — either by another character commenting, or by the narrative slowing down
- **Not be reversed** without dramatic weight (a character who slips into 반말 and then reverts to 존댓말 next scene has done something — fled, regretted, drawn a line)

**Examples of meaningful shifts:**
- 존댓말 → 반말: intimacy claimed, vulnerability shown, or anger unleashed
- 반말 → 존댓말: distance reasserted, betrayal recognized, formality used as a wall
- Inconsistent toggling: a character losing control of who they are in this moment

**A scene to mark each shift:** when a character drops honorifics for the first time, give them a beat afterward — a swallowed breath, a hand stilled mid-gesture, the other character's eyes flicking up. The shift must register.

## 4. 인연 (Yeon) — The Inevitability of Bonds

Coincidence in this aesthetic is not coincidence. Two characters meeting at the same bus stop *was always going to happen*; the universe arranged it. This reframes plot mechanics:

- **Don't apologize for setup.** If two characters need to meet, let the meeting feel fated rather than engineered. Stage it with weather, with a song playing somewhere, with a memory triggered by smell.
- **Foreshadow with objects, not omens.** A character notices a particular book on a stranger's table; chapters later, the stranger is the one they've been searching for. The book was the 인연.
- **Repeat the location.** The same bridge, the same tea house, the same hospital waiting room — across years. Place is the seam where 인연 surfaces.

## 5. Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why it flattens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Over-explaining emotional beats | Removes the reader's work | Let dialogue and action carry it; trust the reader |
| Treating Korean melodrama as the goal | Melodrama amplifies what is already said; jeong/han operate by *understatement* | Cut the lines that explain what the character feels; keep the lines that show what they do *instead* of feeling |
| Tragedy as solution | Killing a character to provoke emotion is the lazy version | The harder version: keep them alive and unable to say the thing |
| Western "redemption arc" | Often imposes a closure the aesthetic resists | Korean arcs more often end on *acceptance without forgiveness* — the wound stays, but the character can carry it |
| Generic flashback | Triggers without specificity | Anchor every flashback to a *specific sensory detail* — the song, the food, the weather, the exact phrase the parent used |

## 6. Quick Calibration Prompts

When stuck, ask one of these:

- "What is this character refusing to say out loud?"
- "What did this room used to be?"
- "Who is missing from this scene, and how does the absence shape the dialogue?"
- "If this character could say one true sentence to the person they love most, what would it be — and why can't they?"
- "What is the smallest object in this scene, and what does it remember?"
