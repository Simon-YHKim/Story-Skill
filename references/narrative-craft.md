## 1. Narratology (서사학)

### Focalization (Genette)
**Who sees** — distinct from who speaks. Three types:
- **Zero focalization** (omniscient): narrator knows more than any character
- **Internal focalization** (limited): narrator knows what one character knows; can shift between characters but only one at a time
- **External focalization** (objective/cinematic): narrator reports only observable behavior

**Game-relevant**: most game narratives use shifting internal focalization (player as one character) plus environmental external (the world as silent witness). Shifts in focalization are choices — make them deliberate.

### Narrative Time vs. Story Time
- **Story time**: chronological events as they happen in the world
- **Narrative time**: the order, duration, and frequency of presentation
- **Order**: prolepsis (flash-forward), analepsis (flashback) — used to plant or recover
- **Duration**: scene (story=narrative), summary (story>narrative), pause (story<narrative — description, internal moment), ellipsis (gap)
- **Frequency**: singular (told once), repetitive (told multiple times — almost always significant — same event from new POV reveals what was missed)

### Distance
The "narrative distance" between narrator and character interior:
- **Close**: character's exact thoughts, sensory experience
- **Medium**: paraphrased interior, narrator's framing
- **Far**: only external observation
A character's emotional intensity is often best handled by *increasing* distance, not decreasing it. The reader's imagination does more work than direct prose can.

### Unreliable Narration
Categories (Phelan):
- **Misreporting** (gets facts wrong)
- **Misinterpreting** (reads correctly but reasons badly)
- **Misevaluating** (sees and reasons but values wrongly)
A great unreliable narrator is *consistent in their unreliability* — the reader can decode the bias.

### Frame Narratives
Stories within stories. Use when the act of telling is itself the subject — the frame should *do something* (reveal the teller, recontextualize, set up dramatic irony). A frame that just delivers content with extra steps is dead weight.

## 2. Dramatic Theory (극작법)

### Aristotelian Foundations (Poetics)
- **Mimesis**: imitation of action — drama shows, doesn't explain
- **Catharsis**: emotional purgation — pity and fear, then release
- **Hamartia**: the protagonist's flaw / error — not necessarily a moral failing, often a misjudgment that the character could not have known to avoid
- **Peripeteia**: reversal — the moment fortune turns, ideally tied to a recognition
- **Anagnorisis**: recognition — the protagonist sees what they could not see before; best when *the audience and protagonist recognize together* or when the audience has known and the protagonist finally catches up

### The Three Unities (often broken intentionally)
Time, place, action. Most modern stories break two; knowing which two — and why — focuses craft.

### Egri's Premise (Lajos Egri, *The Art of Dramatic Writing*)
Every well-made story can be reduced to one declarative sentence: **"[Force] leads to [Consequence]."**
- "Great love defies even death." (Romeo & Juliet)
- "Ruthless ambition leads to its own destruction." (Macbeth)
- "분노는 가장 사랑한 것을 파괴한다."

If you cannot write the premise, the story has no spine. Once written, every scene must either prove, complicate, or test the premise.

### Dramatic Irony
The audience knows something a character does not. Used well, this is the most powerful tension generator in drama. Used poorly, it makes the character look stupid. The difference: dramatic irony works when the character *would not reasonably know* what we know — not when they failed to notice the obvious.

### Reversal & Recognition (Aristotle's gold standard)
The strongest scenes combine peripeteia and anagnorisis in one moment: the character realizes the truth *and* their fortune turns *because of the realization*. Plant for this carefully.

## 3. Beat Structures (구조 모델)

Pick one consciously per project. State which and why in the synopsis.

### 3-Act (Aristotelian / Hollywood)
Setup → Confrontation → Resolution. Inciting incident at ~12%, midpoint reversal at ~50%, crisis at ~75%, climax at ~90%. **Best for**: action-driven plots, mainstream cinema, most western RPGs.

### 5-Act (Freytag / Korean 발단·전개·위기·절정·결말)
Exposition → Rising → Crisis → Climax → Falling/Resolution. The Korean form gives more weight to 결말 (the falling/aftermath) than the Hollywood 3-act. **Best for**: relational dramas, character studies, slower-burn arcs.

### Kishōtenketsu (起承転結)
4 movements:
- **Ki (起)**: introduction — establishes the world and characters
- **Shō (承)**: development — deepens, complicates
- **Ten (転)**: twist — *not* a conflict resolution but a *recontextualization* — a new angle that makes everything before mean differently
- **Ketsu (結)**: conclusion — synthesis

**Notable**: kishōtenketsu does not require external conflict. **Best for**: contemplative pieces, meditative game narratives (Outer Wilds, Spiritfarer), small-scale character work.

### Save the Cat (Blake Snyder) — 15 beats
Opening Image, Theme Stated, Setup, Catalyst, Debate, Break into Two, B Story, Fun and Games, Midpoint, Bad Guys Close In, All Is Lost, Dark Night of the Soul, Break into Three, Finale, Final Image. **Best for**: studio-friendly mainstream works; useful as a checklist even when you break it.

### Hero's Journey (Campbell / Vogler)
Ordinary World → Call → Refusal → Mentor → Threshold → Tests → Approach → Ordeal → Reward → Road Back → Resurrection → Return. **Caveat**: this is a *pattern* observed in myths, not a recipe. Slavish application produces generic fantasy. Use selectively for mythic tones.

### Story Circle (Dan Harmon's simplified Hero's Journey)
8 steps — *You, Need, Go, Search, Find, Take, Return, Change.* Compact and useful for episodic structure. **Best for**: episodic side stories, recurring quest patterns.

### Comparison cheat sheet
| Need | Use |
|---|---|
| Pure plot drive | 3-act |
| Relational depth + aftermath | 5-act / 발단·전개·위기·절정·결말 |
| Recontextualization rather than conflict | Kishōtenketsu |
| Mythic / archetypal | Hero's Journey (carefully) |
| Episodic side content | Story Circle |
| Studio-pleasing checklist | Save the Cat |

## 4. Character Craft (캐릭터 작법)

### Want vs. Need (Robert McKee)
- **Want**: the conscious goal the character would name. External, often misdirected.
- **Need**: the unconscious transformation required for the character to be whole. Internal.
- The arc lives in the gap. Rare exception: stories where the character's want *is* their need (rare and difficult; most fall flat).

### The Lie They Believe (Truby)
The false belief installed by the wound. The character's defining behavior is shaped by trying to be true to this lie.
- Example: "사랑하는 것은 결국 잃는 것이다" → character avoids attachment, sabotages closeness
- The arc: either the lie is dismantled (positive arc), the character clings to it harder (negative arc / tragic), or holds it but the world changes around them (flat arc).

### The Ghost (Trottier / various)
The wound itself, often shown in a backstory event referenced obliquely. Best practice: never fully shown until late, but sensed throughout in avoidance behaviors and triggered reactions.

### Arc Types
- **Positive arc**: lie dismantled, character grows. Most common in mainstream.
- **Negative arc**: lie strengthened, character falls. Tragedy, prestige drama. (Walter White, Macbeth.)
- **Flat arc**: character holds truth and changes the world around them. Mentor figures, certain heroes (Atticus Finch, Captain America). Often paired with a positive-arc deuteragonist.
- **Disillusionment arc**: character starts believing a true thing, comes to believe a darker truer thing. (Many noir protagonists.)

### Internal vs. External Conflict
Every scene should ideally have both. External: the obstacle in the world. Internal: the contradiction in the character. When they align, you have efficient drama. When they diverge intentionally, you have layered drama. When they diverge accidentally, you have confused drama.

### Character vs. Caricature
A character has contradictions that resolve only through specific lived experience. A caricature has traits stacked on a label. Test: can you write a paragraph from this character's POV that *no other character in the cast could write*? If yes — character. If no — caricature.

## 5. Directorial Vocabulary (연출 도구)

### Mise-en-scène (미장센)
Everything visible in the frame: blocking, props, costume, lighting, set dressing. The director's claim that **everything in the frame is meaningful** is the live-action equivalent of "decoration is sin." For game writing, translate to: every described element of a scene/area/cutscene exists for a reason.

### Montage / Editing Theory
- **Eisenstein**: meaning emerges from *collision* of shots — A + B = C, where C is not present in A or B alone
- **Kuleshov effect**: the same face means different things depending on what we cut to next; meaning is in the assembly
- **Continuity editing** (Hollywood standard): smooth invisibility, viewer absorbed
- **Disjunctive editing**: rhythmic dissonance to provoke awareness (Godard, contemporary streaming)

For game narrative: scene order shapes meaning the same way. The information you withhold or front-load *creates* the story even when content is identical.

### Diegesis vs. Mimesis
- **Diegesis**: telling, narrating, summarizing
- **Mimesis**: showing, dramatizing, scene-level rendering
Most weak prose over-uses diegesis ("She was sad about her father"); most weak film over-uses mimesis at the cost of meaning. The skill is calibrating each per beat.

### Blocking and Framing
The physical positions of characters in a scene reveal interior. Two characters facing each other = direct confrontation. Side-by-side = alliance or shared experience. One looking out a window while the other speaks = dissociation, refusal, desire elsewhere. Game cutscene direction borrows this language wholesale.

### The Rule of Threes
In comedy: setup, setup, payoff. In drama: foreshadow, echo, recover. In design: three appearances of an object before it carries narrative weight. The number isn't magic — it's the cognitive minimum for "pattern recognized."

## 6. Game-Narrative Specific (게임 서사 이론)

### Ludonarrative Harmony / Dissonance (Hocking)
- **Harmony**: gameplay verbs and narrative themes align (a stealth game about shame works because the verbs of avoidance match the theme)
- **Dissonance**: gameplay contradicts story (a shooter protagonist who narratively grieves every death; thousands of bodies vs. one cutscene tear)
- For non-trivial games, design narrative such that core player verbs *carry* thematic weight. The verb is the theme.

### Environmental Storytelling
Story told through *space* rather than character or dialogue. The room with two place-settings and one chair pushed back. The arrow embedded in the door. The scratched-out word on the journal.
- Best practice: every environmental detail should answer "what happened here?" *and* "what does it mean for the player to be the one finding this?"
- Souls-like masters of this: FromSoftware, Outer Wilds, Disco Elysium

### Item Description as Lore Vehicle
A line on a weapon's flavor text can carry more story than a cutscene. **Constraint**: the writing must reward close attention. Lazy item flavor breaks immersion faster than missing flavor.

### Branching vs. Authored Arcs
- **Heavy branching** (Detroit, choice-driven VNs): player choices materially change story shape. Risk: shallow per-branch depth, combinatorial explosion.
- **Authored arc with branched flavoring** (most prestige RPGs): main beats fixed, player choices texture *how* beats are reached. Better depth-per-effort.
- **Emergent narrative** (Dwarf Fortress, RimWorld): no authored story, narrative emerges from systems. Different beast — requires designed *generators* not stories.

### Player Agency vs. Authored Drama
The unsolved tension of game narrative. The player wants to feel agentic; the story wants its planned reveals. Design language for managing this:
- **Tutorial agency**: early choices feel large but are equivalent
- **Aesthetic choice**: meaningful-feeling decisions that change texture not direction
- **Convergent branching**: paths diverge then re-converge at planned beats
- **Late-game divergence**: real branching at endings, when accumulated content is small

### The "Verb-as-Theme" Mapping
The game's *primary verbs* should map to the *thematic question*. A game about responsibility should have core verbs of choosing, refusing, carrying. A game about discovery should have looking, listening, piecing-together. When the verbs don't match the theme, the player experience contradicts the story claim.

## 7. Cross-Medium Master Techniques

These techniques recur across novelists, screenwriters, and game writers — they are not medium-specific. They are detailed in `references/master-philosophy.md`; this section gives the working summary.

### Embodied Metaphor (after Han Kang)
For any abstract thematic element (trauma, oppression, faith, decay, memory, regret), find the **physical/biological metaphor** that lets the abstraction live in the body of the prose. The metaphor must be *materially specific to the world*, not borrowed cliché.

- Generic: "The war left lasting trauma."
- Embodied (Han Kang's *Human Acts*): "The torture was like radiation exposure — it lodges in the marrow, alters chromosomes, mutates descendants for decades."
- The embodied version recruits the reader's *body*, not just intellect. It also unifies disparate scenes: once "radiation" is the metaphor, every aftermath scene inherits the radiation logic.

**Rule**: do not write the abstract noun. Find the physical thing it is most like *in this world*, and let the world describe itself in those terms.

### The Sorkin Condition (Intention and Obstacle)
**Every scene with two or more characters must establish, before any dialogue is written**:
- *What does Character A want from this scene specifically?* (Not "to be happy" — something they could fail at right now.)
- *What does Character B want?* (Same constraint.)
- *What is the obstacle?* (May be the other character, the room, the time, the social cost of speaking.)

If A and B want the same thing, or either wants nothing specific, **the scene is dead before dialogue is written**. Subtext, rhythm, register, and silence are layered *on top of* this collision; without the collision they are decoration.

**Diagnostic**: if you swap any line of dialogue between two characters and the scene still works, the characters are not in distinct intentions. Each line should be unmovable from its speaker.

### Pathological Self-Justification (after Dostoevsky)
For any character making a morally compromised choice, the writer must be able to articulate the character's *internal justification* — the chain of reasoning that lets the character keep operating after the act. The reasoning must be **almost convincing**, not strawman.

This is implemented via the **Self-Justification Monologue** worksheet in `references/character-deep-build.md`. Use it as a diagnostic: a character who cannot deliver a self-justification monologue in their own voice is not yet built.

### Index-Card Causality (after Gilligan)
Every plot beat must pass two questions before placement:
1. *What's in this character's head right now?* (motivated by interior state, not authorial convenience)
2. *What happens next because of this?* (causally generates the next beat)

A beat that fails either question is cut. The Setup-Payoff Matrix in `references/setup-payoff.md` is this technique digitalized.

### Co-Writing and Designed Absence (after Morrison and Miyazaki)
The audience reconstructs what is left unsaid. This is **Principle 5** of the skill; see `references/master-philosophy.md` sections 2.2 and 3.1 for full technique. Working summary:
- Trauma is staged at sensory distance, never described directly
- Lore is delivered in fragments (item descriptions, environmental details, NPC asides) that can be triangulated
- The **fragments-to-lore ratio** for a Souls-like is roughly 1:5 — write five times more lore privately than the player will see directly

### Failure as Content (after Kurvitz)
Every fail state, refused offer, missed check, or "do nothing" choice must produce *new narrative material*, not lock-out. The player who roleplays poorly is offering the writer a different story to tell. Write the failure path as if it were canonical.

## 8. Working Heuristics

These are not theory — they are field rules.

- **The first scene should make a promise the last scene keeps.** Reread your opening after writing your ending; cut anything in the opening that the ending does not pay back.
- **Every scene must change something.** If the situation at the end of the scene is identical to the start, cut the scene or add a change.
- **Every named character must want something specific in every scene they appear in.** Otherwise they are decoration.
- **If two scenes do the same work, keep the better one.** Redundancy is the most common bloat.
- **Trust the audience.** Subtract one explanation, then another. Stop when the meaning becomes ambiguous in a *bad* way (not in a productive way).
- **The hardest cut is the line you wrote yourself smiling.** It is often the line that is performing for the writer, not serving the work.
- **Read the dialogue out loud.** Anything that does not survive being spoken aloud is wrong.
- **Name the genre, then break exactly one rule.** Pure genre is forgettable; rule-broken genre is memorable; multiple-rule-broken genre is incoherent.
